Weekly Notes: BNS Transition Rules, E-Challan Proof, and Modern 'Mens Rea'

Hey everyone, let's break down this week's top legal updates. We are looking at how courts are handling the jump from CrPC to BNSS, why police need video proof for dangerous driving challans, and how old maxims apply to new cyber scams.

1. The BNSS Transition & Default Bail

We all know the shift from the old IPC and CrPC to the new Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) and BNSS is a massive deal. But in practice, trial courts have been confused about one major thing: What happens if a crime was committed last year (under IPC), but the police start investigating or arrest the person today? Which law applies?

The Supreme Court made it pretty simple this week. They reminded us of a basic rule of law: substantive law (what defines the crime) looks to the past, but procedural law (how the police investigate) looks to the present. Since Article 20(1) of the Constitution protects people from retroactive laws, the police have to charge the accused under the old IPC for old crimes.

However, the process is completely new. If the investigation starts after the new laws came into play, the police must follow the BNSS rules for search, seizure, and arrest. The old CrPC goes out the window for the investigation part.

Exam Tip

A big topic right now is "default bail." Under the old CrPC Sec 167(2), if police didn't file a charge sheet in 60/90 days, you got bail. The new BNSS changes how police custody is calculated. But the Supreme Court clarified that the right to default bail is tied to Article 21 (Right to Life and Liberty). The transition phase can't be used as an excuse by the State to keep people locked up longer without a charge sheet.

2. E-Challans: Courts Demand Actual Proof

If you drive in any major city right now, you know AI traffic cameras are everywhere. They are sending out automated e-challans left and right. But is a single photo enough to prove you were driving dangerously? Several High Courts have stepped in to say "No."

Let's look at Section 184 of the Motor Vehicles Act (Dangerous Driving). A photo can prove you crossed a stop line, but it can't prove you were driving recklessly. Courts are now ordering that if the traffic police want to fine someone for dangerous driving using AI, they must have a 5 to 10-second video clip showing the actual reckless weaving or danger to others. A still photo isn't enough anymore.

On the flip side, things got stricter for commercial vehicles under Section 194 (Overloading). Instead of sending paper notices, the government has linked the weight sensors at toll plazas directly to the RTO database. If a truck is caught overloading twice, the system will automatically suspend its transport permit digitally for 30 days. No manual paperwork required.

3. 'Mens Rea' in Modern Cyber Scams

We all studied "Actus Non Facit Reum Nisi Mens Sit Rea" in our first year. An act doesn't make someone guilty unless their mind is also guilty. But how does this apply to modern cybercrime?

These days, scammers don't use their own bank accounts. They trick innocent people (like students or daily wagers) into giving up their bank details, or they just hack them. These are called "Mule Accounts." When the police track the stolen money, they freeze these mule accounts and often file FIRs against the account holders for cheating and conspiracy.

Recent judgments are finally correcting this. Courts are saying that just because the stolen money landed in your account (the Actus Reus), it doesn't mean you are a criminal. The police have to prove that you actually knew about the scam (the Mens Rea). If someone just fell for a phishing link, they are a victim, not a co-conspirator.

The 'Willful Blindness' Exception

There is a catch, though. If a student rents out their bank account to a stranger for ₹2000 a month, knowing something shady is going on but choosing to ignore it, the court calls this "Willful Blindness." In that case, the law assumes you had criminal intent, and the Mens Rea defense won't save you.
