Colonial Legacy

The 1860 Origin

Indian Penal Code (IPC) was drafted in 1860 based on the recommendations of the first Law Commission of India, chaired by Thomas Babington Macaulay. It was created by the British to have a uniform criminal law across India, replacing various religious and local penal systems that existed during the Mughal and Princely eras.

Structure

Key Provisions

The IPC originally contained 511 sections divided into 23 chapters. It covers a vast range of offenses—from crimes against the State (Sedition) and public tranquility to offenses affecting the human body (Murder, Rape) and property (Theft, Robbery). Its clarity in defining "Mens Rea" (Guilty Mind) remains a masterpiece of legal drafting.

Evolution

Current Standing

While IPC has governed Indian courts for over 160 years, it is now transitioning into the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS). However, for any crime committed before the full implementation of the new laws, IPC remains the governing statute. It is the most cited legal document in Indian criminal history.
