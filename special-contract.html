Commercial Transactions

Beyond General Principles

"The specialized rules governing agency, bailment, sales, and partnerships."

While the first part of the Indian Contract Act deals with general principles, Special Contracts dive into specific commercial relationships. This subject covers Sections 124-238 of the Indian Contract Act, along with two major independent acts that were originally part of it: The Sale of Goods Act, 1930 and the Indian Partnership Act, 1932.

10 Key Pillars of Special Contracts:

  • Contract of Indemnity (Sec 124).
  • Contract of Guarantee & Surety.
  • Bailment (Sec 148) & Finder of Goods.
  • Pledge by mercantile agents.
  • Creation and termination of Agency.
  • Sale vs. Agreement to Sell (SOGA).
  • Conditions & Warranties.
  • Rights of an Unpaid Seller.
  • Mutual Agency in Partnership.
  • Dissolution of a Partnership Firm.

Statutory Breakdown

Core Doctrines

Commercial law heavily relies on implied terms, mutual trust, and the specific transfer of risk and property across these three distinct domains of special contracts.

5 Landmark Legal Concepts:
Indemnity vs. Guarantee

Indemnity involves two parties (saving from loss). Guarantee involves three parties (Principal Debtor, Creditor, Surety) to perform a promise if a third person defaults.

Nemo Dat Quod Non Habet

"No one gives what they do not have." A core rule in the Sale of Goods Act regarding the transfer of title by a non-owner, subject to certain exceptions.

Caveat Emptor

"Let the buyer beware." The principle that the buyer alone is responsible for checking the quality and suitability of goods before a purchase is made.

True Test of Partnership

Sharing of profits is only prima facie evidence. The true test is Mutual Agency—whether the business is carried on by all or any of them acting for all.

Doctrine of Holding Out

If a person represents themselves as a partner, or allows themselves to be represented as one, they are liable to anyone who gives credit to the firm on that faith.

Evolution

Key Case Laws

Judicial interpretations have shaped the boundaries of liability for agents, sureties, and partners, balancing the rigidity of commercial contracts with equitable principles.

"The law of special contracts is the invisible architecture of commerce. It dictates who bears the loss when goods perish, agents go rogue, or partners fall out."

5 Essential Jurisprudence Points:

  • Cox v. Hickman: The foundational English case establishing that 'mutual agency', not mere profit-sharing, is the true test of a partnership.
  • Gajanan Moreshwar v. Moreshwar Madan: Clarified that an indemnifier's liability commences as soon as the liability of the indemnified becomes absolute and certain.
  • Amrit Lal Goverdhan Lalan v. State Bank of Travancore: Established the rights of a surety to the securities held by the creditor, and discharge of surety if securities are lost.
  • Lee v. Griffin: Differentiated between a 'contract for sale of goods' and a 'contract for work and labour'.
  • Snow White Food Products v. Sohan Lal: Addressed the implied authority of a partner and the binding nature of their acts on the entire firm.
