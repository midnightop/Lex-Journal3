Market Regulation

The Anti-Trust Framework

"Protecting the process of competition, not necessarily the competitors."

Replacing the outdated MRTP Act, the Competition Act, 2002 reflects India's shift towards a free-market economy. Its primary goals are to prevent practices having an Appreciable Adverse Effect on Competition (AAEC), promote and sustain market competition, protect consumer interests, and ensure freedom of trade carried on by other participants in markets in India.

10 Key Pillars of Competition Law:

  • Anti-Competitive Agreements (Sec 3).
  • Abuse of Dominant Position (Sec 4).
  • Regulation of Combinations (Sec 5 & 6).
  • Establishment of the CCI (Sec 7).
  • Role of the Director General (DG).
  • Determining the "Relevant Market".
  • Cartelization & Bid Rigging penalties.
  • Leniency Programmes for whistleblowers.
  • Extraterritorial Jurisdiction of CCI.
  • Appeals to NCLAT & Supreme Court.

Core Concepts

Regulating Monopoly

The Act does not punish a company merely for being large or dominant; it strictly punishes the *abuse* of that dominance to drive out rivals or exploit consumers.

5 Landmark Legal Doctrines:
Relevant Market

The starting point of any anti-trust investigation. Comprises the "Relevant Product Market" (interchangeable goods) and "Relevant Geographic Market" (conditions of competition).

Appreciable Adverse Effect on Competition (AAEC)

The threshold test under Section 3 to determine if an agreement (horizontal or vertical) harms market competition. Cartels are presumed to have AAEC.

Predatory Pricing

Selling goods below the cost of production with the clear intent to eliminate competitors, which is a classic abuse of a dominant position under Section 4.

Combinations (M&A)

Mergers, acquisitions, or amalgamations that cross a certain financial threshold must seek prior approval from the CCI to ensure they don't create a monopoly.

Gun Jumping

When merging parties fail to notify the CCI of a reportable combination or implement the merger before the CCI's waiting period expires.

Judicial Compendium

10 Landmark Judgments

01.

Belaire Owners Association v. DLF Limited

Defining "Dominant Position" in the real estate sector and penalizing unfair conditions in buyer agreements.

02.

CCI v. Steel Authority of India (SAIL)

Supreme Court ruling that Section 26(1) orders are administrative/internal and do not require prior hearing of parties.

03.

Excel Crop Care Ltd. v. CCI

Introduced the principle of "Relevant Turnover" for calculating penalties to ensure proportionality.

04.

Builders Association of India v. Cement Manufacturers

The famous "Cement Cartel" case proving price parallelism and coordinated production limits among 11 major firms.

05.

Google Android Case (CCI 2022)

Landmark tech ruling against mandatory pre-installation of apps and stifling of "Android Forks."

06.

Shamsher Kataria v. Honda Siel Cars India

Addressed the "aftermarket" dominance in spare parts, making it easier for local garages to compete.

07.

Uber India v. CCI (Supreme Court)

Explored the concept of "Predatory Pricing" in the taxi-aggregator market and the requirement of investigation.

08.

Coal India Ltd. v. CCI (2023)

SC ruled that even Public Sector Undertakings (PSUs) are subject to Competition Law despite their social mandates.

09.

Rajasthan Cylinders v. CCI

Clarified that parallel pricing alone isn't enough to prove a cartel if the market is monopsonistic (single buyer).

10.

Monsanto v. CCI

Ruled that CCI has jurisdiction to investigate royalty matters even if they fall under Patent Law/IPR.
