Civil Wrongs

Torts, MV Act & Consumer Law

"Where there is a right, there is a remedy."

The Law of Torts deals with civil wrongs independent of contract, where the remedy is a common-law action for unliquidated damages. Because Torts are largely uncodified in India, this subject integrates two highly codified and practical statutory frameworks: The Motor Vehicles Act, 1988 (for accident claims) and the Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (for product liability and service deficiency).

10 Key Pillars of the Subject:

  • Negligence & Duty of Care.
  • Nuisance (Public & Private).
  • Defamation (Libel & Slander).
  • Vicarious Liability (Master/Servant).
  • General Defenses (Volenti non fit injuria).
  • Strict & Absolute Liability.
  • MV Act: No-Fault Liability (Sec 140/164).
  • Hit and Run Compensation.
  • CPA 2019: Deficiency in Service.
  • Product Liability & E-commerce Rules.

Core Maxims & Statutes

Doctrines of Liability

Liability in tort arises from the breach of a duty primarily fixed by law. The MV Act and CPA operationalize these principles through specialized tribunals and commissions to ensure rapid justice.

5 Landmark Legal Concepts:
Injuria Sine Damno / Damnum Sine Injuria

Legal injury without actual damage is actionable (e.g., Ashby v. White). Actual damage without legal injury is not actionable (e.g., Gloucester Grammar School case).

Res Ipsa Loquitur

"The thing speaks for itself." A rule of evidence shifting the burden of proof in negligence cases where the accident could not have happened without negligence.

Strict vs. Absolute Liability

Strict Liability (Rylands v. Fletcher) has exceptions like Act of God. Absolute Liability (M.C. Mehta case) applies to inherently dangerous industries with no exceptions.

No-Fault Liability (MV Act)

Allows victims of motor accidents to claim basic compensation without having to prove the negligence or fault of the driver.

Unfair Trade Practice (CPA)

A trade practice which, for the purpose of promoting the sale or use of any goods or services, adopts any unfair method or deceptive practice.

Evolution

Key Case Laws

Because the Indian Law of Torts is largely based on English common law tailored to Indian conditions, judicial precedents are the very lifeblood of the subject, establishing duties of care from ginger beer bottles to medical operation theaters.

"The categories of negligence are never closed. The law must continuously evolve to hold the negligent accountable, whether on a highway or in a hospital."

5 Essential Jurisprudence Points:

  • Donoghue v. Stevenson: The "Snail in the Bottle" case that established the modern tort of negligence and the 'Neighbour Principle'.
  • M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Oleum Gas Leak): Evolved the rule of Absolute Liability in India, departing from the English strict liability rule.
  • Kasturi Lal v. State of UP: A landmark (though widely criticized) case regarding the State's sovereign immunity and vicarious liability.
  • Indian Medical Association v. V.P. Shantha: Brought medical professionals providing paid services under the ambit of the Consumer Protection Act.
  • Sarla Verma v. DTC: Standardized the multiplier method for calculating compensation in Motor Vehicle accident claims.
