The Death of IPC 302: How Section 103 BNS Redefines Murder in 2026

Rey

Surendranath Law College | Calcutta University

If you have spent any time in an Indian courtroom or even watching a legal drama, the term 302 was synonymous with the ultimate crime. But as we navigate 2026, the Indian Penal Code (IPC) has officially been retired to the archives of history. In its place stands the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS). For legal practitioners, students, and the general public, the shift from IPC 302 to Section 103 BNS is the most significant statutory change of our generation.

1. Section 103(1): The New Home for Murder

Section 103(1) of the BNS is the direct replacement for the old Section 302. While the transition might seem like a simple renumbering exercise, the intent behind the BNS was to simplify the language and make the code more Indian in its application. However, the gravity of the offense remains untouched.

What constitutes Murder now?

The definition of murder is now found in Section 101 BNS. If a person causes death with the intention of causing death, or causing such bodily injury that they know is likely to result in death, they are charged under Section 103.

The Punishment: Death Penalty or Imprisonment for Life, and a mandatory fine.

2. The Mob Lynching Clause: Section 103(2)

This is where the BNS truly evolves beyond the British-era IPC. For years, the Indian judiciary struggled to deal with Mob Lynching using fragmented sections like Unlawful Assembly. In 2026, we finally have a standalone statutory weapon against group-based violence.

How Section 103(2) Works:

If a group of five or more persons acts in concert to commit murder based on the victims identity be it Race, Caste, Community, Sex, Place of Birth, or Language every member of that crowd is held equally liable for murder.

"The BNS has removed the safety in numbers excuse. If you are part of a mob that takes a life, the law views you as a murderer, plain and simple."

3. What Happens to Old IPC 302 Cases?

One of the most frequent questions arises about the pending cases. If a crime was committed before the BNS came into force, will it be converted to Section 103? The answer lies in the principle of Non-Retroactivity. CrPC and IPC will still apply to trials for offenses committed before the transition date. However, all new FIRs registered in 2026 are being filed strictly under BNS 103.

4. Survival of Precedents

Law students often fear that renumbering laws means throwing away decades of case law. That is a myth. The Rarest of Rare doctrine established in Bachan Singh v State of Punjab remains the gold standard for judges deciding between Life Imprisonment and the Death Penalty under Section 103 BNS. The philosophy of the court has not changed, only the statutory tool has.

Pro-Tip for Drafting

When drafting your first few BNS based petitions, make sure to cross reference the IPC sections in brackets for the first year. It helps the court transition smoothly. Use the format: Section 103 BNS (erstwhile Section 302 IPC).
