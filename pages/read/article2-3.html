Current Case Study: April 2026

Raghav Chadha & 6 Others Join BJP

The recent shift of 7 out of 10 AAP Rajya Sabha MPs including Raghav Chadha, Harbhajan Singh, and Swati Maliwal to the BJP has put the 10th Schedule back in the spotlight. This move is a textbook example of how the "2/3rd merger rule" is utilized in modern politics to avoid disqualification.

1. What Exactly Is the 10th Schedule?

Introduced by the 52nd Amendment in 1985, the Anti Defection Law sets the rules for when a Member of Parliament or a State Legislature can be disqualified for switching parties.

Four Key Grounds for Disqualification:

  • Voluntary Exit: Resigning from your party after being elected on its ticket.
  • Defying the Whip: Voting against your party's orders without prior permission.
  • Independent Move: An independent member joining any political party after the election.
  • Nominated Wait: A nominated member joining a party after 6 months of taking their seat.

2. The 2/3rd Merger Rule

In the past, a 1/3rd split was allowed. However, the 91st Amendment in 2003 changed the game. Now, for a group of legislators to join another party without losing their seats, they must prove that at least two thirds of the original party's legislative strength has agreed to the merger.

Applying the Math:

In the Raghav Chadha case, since the group consisted of 7 out of 10 members (70%), they exceeded the 66.6% (2/3rd) requirement. This technically classifies their move as a merger rather than defection, protecting their seats in the Rajya Sabha.

The "Wholesale" Defection Problem:

While the law stops individual "retail" defections, it has inadvertently encouraged "wholesale" shifts where large blocks move together to hit that 66% mark. This remains a major point of contention in 2026 legal circles.

"The law was meant to punish the defector, but today it often just changes the price of the defection."

Old Rule (Before 2003) Modern Rule (Post 91st Amendment)
1/3rd members could split 2/3rd members must merge
Split recognized as legal Splits are now illegal
Retail defection easier Wholesale defection required

3. The Power of the Speaker

The Presiding Officer holds the ultimate power to disqualify. However, 2026 has seen a surge in petitions demanding a time limit for these decisions. In the famous Kihoto Hollohan case, the Supreme Court declared that the Speaker acts as a Judge (Tribunal), meaning their decision is open to Judicial Review if it is biased or mala fide.

4. Critical Loopholes to Watch

Modern politicians have found ways to bypass the 10th Schedule. Instead of defecting, many choose to resign from the house entirely. This reduces the total strength of the house, helps the opposing side win a trust vote, and allows the individual to contest again in a by election. This "Resignation over Defection" strategy is currently the biggest challenge for Constitutional experts.

Exam/Practice Note

Always remember that the 10th Schedule does not apply to the Speaker themselves if they decide to resign from their party to maintain neutrality while in office. This is a rare exception designed to protect the dignity of the Chair.
